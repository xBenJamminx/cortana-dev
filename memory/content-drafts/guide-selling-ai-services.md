# How to Sell AI Services to Clients (Even If You're Not Technical)

**By Ben | BuildsByBen**

---

You know how to use AI. You've built workflows, written prompts that actually work, maybe automated a few things in your own life or business. And at some point it hits you: people would pay for this. Not "people" in the abstract. Real business owners who are drowning in tasks that AI could handle in minutes.

The problem isn't whether the demand exists. It does. The problem is that most people who are good with AI have no idea how to package what they know into something a client would pay for. They don't know where to find clients, what to charge, or how to deliver without it turning into a nightmare.

This guide fixes that. It's the full path from "I'm pretty good with AI" to "clients pay me real money to do AI work." Everything here comes from actually doing this -- selling AI services, delivering the work, dealing with scope creep, figuring out pricing the hard way. No theory. No hypotheticals.

Let's get into it.

---

## Section 1: What AI Services You Can Actually Sell

Before you worry about finding clients, you need to know what you're selling. Here are the categories that people consistently pay for, ranked roughly by how easy they are to start offering today.

**Content Systems**
This is the easiest entry point. You help a business create content faster, more consistently, or at higher quality using AI. This could mean building them a prompt library for their brand voice, setting up a content calendar with AI-generated drafts, or creating a full pipeline where raw ideas become platform-ready posts. Every business knows they need to post more. Almost none of them are doing it well.

**AI Audits**
You sit down with a business (or their team) and map out every process they're currently doing manually that AI could handle. Then you give them a prioritized report: here's what to automate first, here's the tool, here's the estimated time savings. This is a fantastic first offering because it's low-risk for the client, fast for you to deliver, and it almost always leads to follow-up work.

**Workflow Automation**
You connect their tools. Maybe their customer inquiries come in through email, get logged in a spreadsheet, and someone manually sends a response template. You replace the manual parts with AI-powered automation using tools like Make, Zapier, or n8n. The client doesn't care how it works. They care that their inbox isn't a full-time job anymore.

**Research and Analysis**
A lot of businesses need someone to process large amounts of information -- competitor analysis, market research, customer feedback analysis, trend monitoring. AI is absurdly good at this. You can deliver in hours what used to take a VA a week. The deliverable is usually a report or dashboard, which is easy to package and easy for clients to understand.

**Chatbots and Customer-Facing AI**
Building chatbots for websites, internal knowledge bases for teams, or AI assistants that handle customer questions. This requires slightly more technical comfort, but tools like Voiceflow, Botpress, or even custom GPTs make it accessible. Clients love this because they can see it working immediately.

**Custom Prompts and Prompt Libraries**
Some businesses just need someone to write really good prompts for their specific use cases. Sales teams that need email templates. Support teams that need response frameworks. Marketing teams that need ad copy generators. You write the prompts, test them, document how to use them, and hand them over.

**The key insight:** You don't need to offer all of these. Pick one or two that match your skills and interests, get good at delivering those, and expand later. Trying to be "the AI person who does everything" is a fast way to deliver mediocre work and burn out.

## Section 2: Finding Clients

Clients aren't hiding. They're complaining in public about the exact problems you can solve. You just need to know where to listen.

**Where they hang out:**

- **LinkedIn.** This is the richest hunting ground right now. Small business owners, marketing directors, ops managers -- they're all posting about being overwhelmed, about wanting to "leverage AI" but not knowing where to start. Join groups around small business, marketing, and operations. Comment thoughtfully on posts about productivity and AI. Don't pitch. Just be helpful.
- **Local business groups.** Facebook groups for local businesses, your city's Chamber of Commerce, BNI groups, Nextdoor business threads. These people are usually less tech-savvy and more willing to pay someone to handle the AI side for them.
- **Industry-specific communities.** Real estate agents, lawyers, dentists, fitness coaches, e-commerce sellers -- every industry has forums, Slack groups, Facebook groups, and subreddits. Pick one industry and go deep.
- **Upwork and Fiverr.** Not glamorous, but real. Search for "AI automation," "ChatGPT," "AI content" and look at what people are already posting as jobs. Even if you don't bid on them, it shows you what the demand looks like.
- **Your existing network.** This is the one everyone ignores. You probably know 5-10 people who run businesses or work at companies that could use AI help. A simple message -- "Hey, I've been getting into AI automation and I'm looking for a few businesses to work with. Know anyone who might be interested?" -- works better than any cold outreach strategy.

**How to spot opportunities:**

Listen for these phrases. They're buying signals:
- "We spend so much time on [repetitive task]"
- "I wish I could clone myself"
- "We tried ChatGPT but couldn't get it to work for our use case"
- "Our team is too small to handle [volume]"
- "I know we should be using AI but I don't know where to start"

When you hear these, don't pitch your services. Ask a follow-up question. "What does that process look like right now?" or "How much time does that eat up per week?" You're gathering intel. The pitch comes later, on a real call, after you understand their actual problem.

**The outreach message that works:**

Don't send essays. Don't list your services. Keep it short and specific:

```
Hey [Name],

I saw your post about [specific problem they mentioned]. I've been helping
businesses automate that exact kind of work using AI -- usually saves
5-10 hours a week depending on volume.

Would you be open to a quick 15-minute call so I can learn more about
how you're handling it now? No pitch, just want to see if there's a fit.

[Your name]
```

That's it. Short. Specific. Low commitment. The "no pitch" line lowers their guard, and the 15-minute window makes it feel easy to say yes.

## Section 3: The Discovery Call Framework

This is where most people screw up. They get on a call and either talk too much about AI (the client doesn't care about the tools) or they don't ask enough questions to actually scope the work.

Here's the framework. Memorize it.

**The first 5 minutes: Build rapport and set the frame**

Don't jump into business immediately. Ask them how their day's going, mention something specific about their business you noticed. Then set expectations:

*"I appreciate you taking the time. Here's how I like to do these -- I'll ask you a bunch of questions about how things work right now, and by the end we'll both know whether it makes sense to work together. Sound good?"*

This does two things: it positions you as a professional who has a process, and it takes pressure off both of you.

**The next 15 minutes: The diagnostic questions**

These are the questions that do the actual work:

1. *"Walk me through how [the problem process] works right now, step by step."*
2. *"How much time does that take per week? Who's doing it?"*
3. *"What happens when it doesn't get done? What's the cost?"*
4. *"Have you tried to fix this before? What happened?"*
5. *"If this was handled perfectly, what would that look like for you?"*
6. *"What's your timeline? Is this urgent or exploratory?"*

Take notes during this. Repeat back what you hear. "So if I'm understanding right, your team spends about 12 hours a week manually sorting these inquiries, and when it backs up, leads go cold. Is that accurate?" Clients love being heard. It also makes scoping way easier.

**The last 5 minutes: Next steps**

Don't price on the call unless it's something dead simple. Instead:

*"I've got a really clear picture of what you need. Let me put together a proposal with exactly what I'd do, the timeline, and pricing. I'll send it over by [specific day]. If it looks good, we move forward. If not, no hard feelings."*

This gives you time to think about the scope without the pressure of making something up on the spot. It also makes you look more professional than the person who blurts out "$2,000" and then realizes the project is actually worth three times that.

## Section 4: Delivering the Work

You landed the client. Now you need to actually deliver. Here's how to do it without overcomplicating things.

**Tools you'll actually use:**

- **Claude or ChatGPT** for the core AI work (content generation, analysis, prompt engineering)
- **Make or Zapier** for connecting apps and automating workflows (no code required)
- **Google Docs/Sheets or Notion** for deliverables and documentation
- **Loom** for recording walkthroughs and tutorials (clients love video explanations)
- **Airtable** for more complex tracking or content pipeline builds

You don't need 20 tools. For most client projects, you need an AI model, a way to connect things, and a way to present the deliverable. That's it.

**How to package deliverables:**

Whatever you build, document it like you're handing it to someone who's never seen it before. Because you are.

Every deliverable should include:
1. **What it does** -- one paragraph, plain English, no jargon
2. **How to use it** -- step-by-step with screenshots or a Loom video
3. **What to do if something breaks** -- common issues and fixes
4. **What's NOT included** -- this protects you from scope creep

A prompt library isn't just a Google Doc full of prompts. It's a branded document with categories, instructions for each prompt, example outputs, and customization notes. A workflow automation isn't just a Zapier connection. It's the automation plus documentation plus a walkthrough video plus a "here's how to modify it" guide.

The documentation is what separates "that person who helped us with AI once" from "our AI consultant." Invest the time.

**Setting expectations:**

Underpromise, overdeliver. Classic advice that people still ignore. Tell them it'll take a week, deliver in 5 days. Tell them you'll provide 10 prompts, deliver 12. Tell them the automation will save 5 hours a week, and when it saves 8, you look like a genius.

Also: set a revision limit. Two rounds of revisions included in the price. After that, it's additional scope. Put this in your proposal. You will thank yourself later.

**The handoff template:**

When you deliver the final work, send something like this:

```
Subject: Your [Project Name] -- Final Delivery

Hey [Name],

Everything's ready. Here's what's included:

- [Deliverable 1] -- [one-line description]
- [Deliverable 2] -- [one-line description]
- Documentation and walkthrough video: [link]

I've also included a quick-start guide so your team can
start using this immediately.

Next steps:
- Review everything and let me know if you have questions
- We have [X] revision rounds included if you need tweaks
- I'll check in next [day] to see how it's going

Excited for you to see the time savings on this one.

[Your name]
```

Clean. Professional. Makes the client feel like they hired someone who has their act together.

## Section 5: Pricing Models

Pricing is where everyone gets weird. They either charge way too little because they feel guilty, or they pull a number out of the air and hope for the best. Here's how to think about it.

**Hourly ($50-150/hr)**

Best for: Ongoing advisory work, consulting calls, small tweaks.

Worst for: Projects where you're fast. If you can build something in 2 hours that saves the client 20 hours a week, hourly pricing punishes you for being good. Only use this when the scope is genuinely unclear or when you're doing ongoing support.

**Project-Based ($500-5,000+)**

Best for: Defined deliverables with clear scope. Content systems, automation builds, AI audits, prompt libraries.

This is where you should live for most of your work. Price based on the value to the client, not the hours it takes you. If an automation saves a 3-person team 10 hours a week, that's worth thousands per month to them. Your price should reflect a fraction of that value, not the 6 hours it took you to build it.

**Monthly Retainer ($500-3,000/mo)**

Best for: Ongoing work with existing clients. Content management, monitoring automations, monthly AI audits, continuous optimization.

Retainers are the goal. They're predictable income and they keep clients long-term. But don't offer retainers to new clients immediately. Do a project first. Prove the value. Then say: "I can keep optimizing and maintaining this for you on a monthly basis." Most clients who are happy with the project work will say yes.

**When to raise prices:**

- When you're booked out more than 2 weeks
- When you haven't raised prices in 3 months
- When new clients say yes without negotiating
- When you realize the value you're delivering far exceeds what you're charging

Raise prices for new clients first. Keep existing clients at their current rate until renewal. Simple.

**Your Starter Package**

This is the most important concept in this section. When you're starting out, you need one offering that's easy to explain, easy to deliver, and priced low enough that it's a no-brainer for the client.

Here's the one I recommend:

**The AI Quick-Win Package ($500-$1,000)**

What's included:
- 60-minute discovery call to identify their top 3 time-wasting processes
- AI implementation for their #1 priority (could be a content system, a prompt library, a simple automation, or an internal chatbot)
- Full documentation and walkthrough video
- 30-minute training call for their team
- 2 weeks of email support after delivery

Why this works: It's low enough that small businesses will say yes without a long sales cycle. It's structured enough that you know exactly what you're delivering. And it almost always leads to more work because once they see one thing automated, they want everything automated.

Frame it like this on calls: *"Most of my clients start with a Quick-Win engagement where we tackle the single biggest time sink in your workflow. It's a fast way for both of us to see if the working relationship is a fit, and you walk away with something useful regardless."*

No one feels locked in. Everyone gets value. And your conversion rate on follow-up work will be high.

## Section 6: Building a Portfolio from Scratch

"But I don't have any clients yet. How do I get clients without a portfolio, and how do I get a portfolio without clients?"

You do free or cheap work first. Strategically.

**Step 1: Pick 2-3 people you know and offer to help for free**

Not random strangers. People whose businesses you understand, who you actually want to help, and who will give you honest feedback. Tell them: "I'm building out my AI services offering and I'd love to use your business as a case study. I'll do the work for free -- all I ask is that you give me a testimonial and let me share the results."

Almost everyone says yes to free. And the results you get become your portfolio.

**Step 2: Document everything obsessively**

Before and after. Screenshots. Time savings. Here's what the process looked like before, here's what it looks like now. Numbers are everything. "Reduced content creation time from 8 hours/week to 2 hours/week" is a portfolio piece. "Helped a business with AI" is not.

Use this template for each case study:

```
## [Client Name or Industry] -- [What You Did]

**The Problem:**
[2-3 sentences. What was the pain? How much time/money was it costing?]

**What I Built:**
[2-3 sentences. What did you deliver? What tools did you use?]

**The Results:**
- [Specific metric: time saved, output increased, cost reduced]
- [Specific metric]
- [Client quote if you have one]

**Tools Used:** [List them]
```

**Step 3: Turn case studies into content**

Every project you do is at least 3-5 pieces of content:
- A case study for your portfolio/website
- A Twitter/LinkedIn post about the result
- A "how I did it" breakdown post
- A lesson learned post
- A prompt or template you can share publicly (sanitized of client details)

This does double duty: it builds your portfolio AND it attracts new clients who see the results and think "I want that for my business."

**Step 4: Create a simple portfolio page**

Doesn't need to be fancy. A Notion page, a Carrd site, or even a well-formatted Google Doc works. Include:
- A one-paragraph description of what you do
- 2-3 case studies using the template above
- A list of services you offer
- How to contact you

Link to this in your social media bios, include it in outreach messages, and update it every time you finish a project.

**Step 5: Ask every client for a referral**

After you deliver and they're happy, send this:

```
Hey [Name],

Really glad this is working well for you. Quick question --
do you know anyone else who's dealing with similar challenges?
I've got a couple spots open this month and I'd love an intro
if anyone comes to mind.

No pressure at all. Just figured I'd ask since you've seen the
work firsthand.
```

Referrals convert at 3-5x the rate of cold outreach. One happy client can fill your calendar for months.

## The Bottom Line

You don't need a computer science degree to sell AI services. You don't need to know how to code. You don't need a perfect website or a massive following. You need to know how to use AI tools well, understand what businesses struggle with, and communicate clearly about how you can help.

Start with the Quick-Win package. Pick one type of service. Find 2-3 people to work with for free or cheap. Document the results. Share them publicly. Ask for referrals.

The people who are making real money with AI services right now aren't the most technical people in the room. They're the ones who figured out how to explain the value in terms the client cares about, deliver clean work with good documentation, and follow up.

That's the whole playbook. Now go use it.
