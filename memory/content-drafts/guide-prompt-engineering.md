## The Problem: Why Most Prompts Fail

Here's a prompt most people would write:

```
Write a blog post about productivity tips for remote workers.
```

And here's what they get: a generic, forgettable, could-have-been-written-by-anyone article that reads like every other productivity listicle on the internet.

The problem isn't the AI. The problem is that this prompt gives the model almost nothing to work with. No audience. No angle. No voice. No structure. No quality criteria. The AI has to guess everything, and when it guesses, it defaults to the most average, middle-of-the-road response possible.

Think of it this way: if you hired a world-class ghostwriter and said "write something about productivity," they'd ask you 15 questions before writing a single word. Who's the audience? What's the angle? What tone? What's the goal? What should the reader do after reading it?

**Prompting IS those 15 questions.** The better your prompt, the fewer guesses the AI makes. The fewer guesses, the better the output.

## The 7 Core Techniques

These are the techniques that separate "write me a thing" prompts from engineered prompts. Each one is backed by research and proven in real-world use. You don't need all 7 in every prompt — but the more you use, the better your output gets.

---

### Technique 1: Role/Persona Priming

**What it is:** Telling the AI to adopt a specific expert identity before it responds.

**Why it works:** When you assign a role, you're not just being creative — you're activating a different distribution of knowledge and reasoning patterns in the model. A "marketing strategist with 15 years of experience" writes differently than a "helpful AI assistant." The model draws on different vocabulary, frameworks, and depth of analysis.

**The amateur version:**
```
Help me write a sales email.
```

**The engineered version:**
```
You are a B2B sales copywriter who has written email sequences
that generated $2M+ in pipeline for SaaS companies. You specialize
in cold outreach that gets replies by leading with the prospect's
problem, not your product. You know that the subject line determines
whether the email gets opened, and the first sentence determines
whether it gets read.

Write a cold sales email for [PRODUCT] targeting [AUDIENCE].
```

**What changed:** The second version doesn't just say "be a copywriter." It gives the model:
- **Specific expertise** (B2B, SaaS, cold outreach)
- **A philosophy** (lead with the prospect's problem)
- **Domain knowledge** (subject line → open rate, first sentence → read rate)

This isn't role-playing. You're programming the model's reasoning lens.

**How to build great personas:**
1. Name the specific role (not "expert" — what KIND of expert?)
2. Quantify their experience (years, clients, projects, results)
3. State their philosophy or approach (what do they believe about their craft?)
4. Include domain-specific knowledge (what do they know that a generalist doesn't?)

**Research note:** The "26 Principled Instructions" paper (Bsharat et al., 2023) found that role assignment significantly improved output quality across all tested LLMs, with larger models benefiting the most.

### Technique 2: Chain-of-Thought Reasoning

**What it is:** Forcing the model to think through a problem step-by-step before giving its final answer.

**Why it works:** LLMs generate text one token at a time. Without explicit reasoning steps, the model often jumps to the first plausible answer. When you force it to think step-by-step, each step builds on the previous one, producing more nuanced, accurate, and thoughtful output. This is especially powerful for analysis, strategy, and any task that requires judgment.

**The amateur version:**
```
What price should I charge for my SaaS product?
```

**The engineered version:**
```
Help me determine the right price for my SaaS product.

Before giving your recommendation, think through these steps:

Step 1: Analyze the value my product delivers. What is the
cost of the problem it solves for the customer?

Step 2: Research comparable products. What do similar tools
charge? What pricing models do they use?

Step 3: Calculate unit economics. What does it cost me to
serve each customer?

Step 4: Determine the "no-brainer" price point where the
value clearly exceeds the cost.

Step 5: Consider pricing psychology — should I anchor high
or start low given my stage?

Step 6: Give your final recommendation with specific numbers
and reasoning.

My product: [DETAILS]
```

**What changed:** Instead of asking for an answer, you're asking for a reasoning process. The model works through the problem systematically, and the final recommendation is grounded in actual analysis rather than a guess.

**When to use chain-of-thought:**
- Any task involving analysis or strategy
- Decision-making prompts (should I do X or Y?)
- Complex creative tasks (the model needs to plan before writing)
- Math or logic problems
- Anything where "thinking about it" would improve a human's answer too

**Pro tip:** You can use phrases like "Think step by step," "Before answering, analyze..." or "Walk through your reasoning before concluding." All of these trigger more deliberate processing.

**Research note:** Chain-of-thought prompting was introduced by Wei et al. (2022) at Google and has been shown to dramatically improve performance on reasoning tasks. It's now considered a fundamental prompt engineering technique.

### Technique 3: Few-Shot Examples

**What it is:** Including concrete input/output examples in your prompt so the model can see exactly what "good" looks like.

**Why it works:** Examples are worth a thousand words of instruction. Instead of describing the style, tone, format, and quality you want, you SHOW it. The model pattern-matches against your examples and produces output that follows the same patterns.

**The amateur version:**
```
Write me 3 tweet variations about my new product launch.
```

**The engineered version:**
```
Write 3 tweet variations about my product launch.

Here are examples of the STYLE and QUALITY I want:

Example input: "AI tool that generates invoice reminders"
Example output 1: "I stopped chasing invoices. Now my AI does it.
  3 clients paid within 24 hours of the first automated nudge."
Example output 2: "Built a tool that sends invoice reminders so I
  don't have to. First month: $12K collected that would've been late."
Example output 3: "Nobody talks about the worst part of freelancing:
  becoming a debt collector for your own work. So I automated it."

Now write 3 variations for MY launch: [PRODUCT DETAILS]
```

**What changed:** The examples set the quality bar. They show the model: use specific numbers, lead with results, keep it conversational, don't use corporate language. You didn't have to describe any of that — the examples communicated it instantly.

**How to build great few-shot examples:**
1. **Show the range** — include 2-3 examples that demonstrate different acceptable approaches
2. **Show good AND bad** — if you include a "bad" example labeled as such, the model learns what to avoid
3. **Match the real task** — examples should be structurally similar to what you're asking for
4. **Use real quality** — if your examples are mediocre, the output will be mediocre

**When to use few-shot examples:**
- Formatting: when you need a specific output structure
- Voice/tone: when "write casually" isn't specific enough
- Quality calibration: when you want to set a high bar
- Classification tasks: when the model needs to understand your categories

**Research note:** Few-shot prompting was one of the original capabilities demonstrated in GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020). Adding even 2-3 examples consistently improves output quality and format adherence.

### Technique 4: Structured Formatting with Delimiters

**What it is:** Using clear section markers, XML-like tags, or formatting symbols to organize your prompt into distinct parts.

**Why it works:** Long, unstructured prompts confuse the model. It doesn't know where your instructions end and your content begins, which parts are context vs. requirements, or what's a constraint vs. a suggestion. Delimiters create clear boundaries. The model processes each section as a distinct unit with a specific purpose.

**The amateur version:**
```
I need you to analyze my competitor's website. Their name is
Acme Corp and they sell project management tools. I want to
know their pricing, positioning, and weaknesses. Here's their
homepage copy: [LONG TEXT]. Also my company does similar things
but for freelancers specifically. Be direct and don't use fluff.
```

**The engineered version:**
```
<system>
You are a competitive intelligence analyst specializing in
SaaS market positioning.
</system>

###TASK###
Analyze a competitor and identify positioning gaps I can exploit.

###MY BUSINESS###
Company: [YOUR COMPANY]
What we do: [DESCRIPTION]
Our target customer: [AUDIENCE]

###COMPETITOR DATA###
Name: Acme Corp
Product: Project management tools
Homepage copy:
[PASTE THEIR COPY HERE]

###REQUIRED OUTPUT###
1. Positioning summary (1 paragraph)
2. Pricing analysis (if visible)
3. Top 3 strengths
4. Top 3 weaknesses or gaps
5. Strategic recommendations for MY business

###CONSTRAINTS###
- Be direct, no filler
- Support claims with specific evidence from their copy
- If information isn't available, say so
```

**What changed:** Every piece of information has a labeled home. The model knows exactly what role to play, what data to analyze, what output to produce, and what rules to follow. Nothing is ambiguous.

**Common delimiter formats:**
- `###SECTION###` — clear, widely used
- `<tags>content</tags>` — XML-style, great for nesting
- `---` — simple separators between sections
- `[BRACKETS]` — for variable placeholders
- `"""content"""` — for quoted material or user input

**Best practices:**
- Separate instructions from content (the model should never confuse your data for your prompt)
- Label your sections with descriptive names
- Put constraints in their own section (they tend to get ignored when buried in paragraphs)
- Use consistent formatting throughout (don't mix styles)

**Research note:** Google's prompt engineering guidelines specifically recommend delimiters for complex prompts, noting they "help the model understand the structure and intent of the prompt more accurately."

### Technique 5: Self-Evaluation and Critique Loops

**What it is:** Asking the model to review, critique, and improve its own output as part of the prompt.

**Why it works:** First drafts — whether human or AI — always have problems. Generic phrasing, unnecessary filler, weak arguments, missed opportunities. When you build a self-critique step into your prompt, the model catches these issues before you see them. It's like having a built-in editor.

**The amateur version:**
```
Write a LinkedIn post about my product launch.
```

**The engineered version:**
```
Write a LinkedIn post about my product launch.

[DETAILS]

After writing the first draft, review it against these criteria:

AUTHENTICITY CHECK:
- Does this sound like a real person or a LinkedIn influencer?
  If it could be posted by anyone, rewrite with more specific details.

SCROLL-STOP CHECK:
- Would the first line make someone stop scrolling? If not,
  rewrite the opening to be more specific or surprising.

FILLER CHECK:
- Is there any sentence that could be removed without losing
  meaning? If yes, remove it.

ENGAGEMENT CHECK:
- Does the ending invite a specific response? "What do you think?"
  is not specific. "What's the one feature you wish your [tool] had?" is.

Output both the first draft AND the revised version with notes
on what you changed.
```

**What changed:** The model doesn't just write — it writes, then reviews its own work against specific quality criteria, then revises. The output is dramatically better because it's gone through an editing pass.

**Types of self-evaluation you can build in:**
- **Quality scoring** — "Rate each variation 1-10 on [criteria] and explain your score"
- **Red team critique** — "Now argue against your own recommendation. What did you miss?"
- **Audience test** — "Read this from the perspective of [target reader]. Would they care? What would they skip?"
- **Originality check** — "Is there anything in this response that's generic enough to appear in any [similar content]? If yes, make it more specific."
- **Constraint verification** — "Check that every requirement from the ###CONSTRAINTS### section is met"

**When to use self-evaluation:**
- Any creative output (writing, headlines, scripts)
- Strategic recommendations (the critique catches blind spots)
- When you find yourself always having to fix the same issues in AI output

**Research note:** The "26 Principled Instructions" paper found that prompts including self-evaluation and iterative refinement produced approximately 50% higher quality output compared to single-pass generation.

### Technique 6: Output Primers and Format Control

**What it is:** Explicitly defining the format, structure, and even the first few words of the AI's response.

**Why it works:** Without format guidance, the model guesses how to structure its response — and it often guesses wrong. Too long, wrong format, inconsistent structure, unnecessary preamble. By specifying the exact output format (or even starting the response for the model), you eliminate format problems entirely and the model focuses its reasoning on content.

**The amateur version:**
```
Compare these three project management tools for me.
```

**The engineered version:**
```
Compare these three project management tools for a freelance
web developer managing 3-5 client projects.

Tools: [TOOL A], [TOOL B], [TOOL C]

Output format — use this EXACT structure:

## Quick Verdict
[One sentence: which tool wins for this specific use case and why]

## Comparison Table
| Feature | Tool A | Tool B | Tool C |
| Price | | | |
| Best for | | | |
| Worst at | | | |
| Learning curve | | | |
| Integrations | | | |

## The Real Differences
[3 paragraphs, one per tool. Not feature lists — OPINIONS.
"Tool A is great if you [X] but frustrating if you [Y]."]

## My Recommendation
[Direct recommendation for the stated use case with reasoning]

Begin your response with: "For a freelancer managing 3-5 clients,"
```

**What changed:** The model knows exactly what format to use, what each section should contain, and even how to start. Zero energy wasted on figuring out structure = all energy on quality content.

**Format control techniques:**
- **Tables** — force structured comparisons
- **Bullet points** — force concise delivery
- **Word/character limits** — "Answer in under 100 words" forces compression
- **JSON/structured data** — perfect for technical outputs
- **Start the response** — "Begin with:" tells the model exactly where to pick up
- **Section templates** — define each section's purpose and expected length

**Pro tip:** If you want concise output, set a word limit. If you want thorough output, set a minimum word count. If you want structured output, provide the template. The model follows format instructions extremely well.

**Research note:** Google's Gemini documentation specifically recommends "output primers" — giving the model the beginning of the expected response — as a technique for steering generation in the right direction.

### Technique 7: Clarifying Questions and Adaptive Prompts

**What it is:** Building prompts that ask the AI to ask YOU questions before generating output, or that adapt their approach based on the input you provide.

**Why it works:** Sometimes you don't have all the information the model needs to give a great answer. Instead of getting a generic response based on assumptions, you can ask the model to identify what's missing and ask for it. This creates an interactive, adaptive prompt that improves with each exchange.

**The amateur version:**
```
Help me plan my marketing strategy.
```

**The engineered version:**
```
I need help building a marketing strategy. Before creating the
strategy, ask me the most important questions you need answered
to give specific, actionable advice.

Rules for your questions:
- Ask no more than 5 questions
- Each question should be specific, not open-ended
- Provide 2-3 example answers for each question so I know
  what kind of detail you need
- After I answer, create the strategy based on my specific
  situation — not generic marketing advice

Start by asking your questions.
```

**What changed:** Instead of the model guessing your business, audience, budget, timeline, and goals, it ASKS. The resulting strategy is tailored to your actual situation instead of being generic advice.

**When to use clarifying questions:**
- Complex tasks with many variables
- When you're not sure what information the model needs
- Consulting-style interactions (diagnosis before prescription)
- When you want the model to act as an advisor, not just an executor

**Advanced version — adaptive branching:**
```
Based on my answers, choose the appropriate approach:
- If budget > $5K/month: focus on paid acquisition channels
- If budget < $1K/month: focus on organic and content marketing
- If timeline < 30 days: focus on quick wins only
- If timeline > 90 days: build a phased strategy with compounding effects
```

This makes the prompt adapt to different situations without you needing to rewrite it.

**Research note:** Principle 14 from the "26 Principled Instructions" paper — asking the model to ask clarifying questions — showed the highest individual improvement of any technique tested, with 100% effectiveness across all models.

## Putting It All Together: The Prompt Engineering Framework

Now that you know the 7 techniques, here's how to combine them into a complete engineered prompt. You don't need all 7 every time — use what the task requires.

### The Framework

```
<system>
[TECHNIQUE 1: PERSONA]
You are a [SPECIFIC ROLE] who has [EXPERIENCE/CREDENTIALS].
You know that [KEY INSIGHT ABOUT THE DOMAIN]. Your approach
is [PHILOSOPHY/METHOD].
</system>

###TASK###
[Clear statement of what you want]

###INPUT###
[TECHNIQUE 4: STRUCTURED DELIMITERS]
[All context, data, and variables clearly labeled]

###PROCESS###
[TECHNIQUE 2: CHAIN-OF-THOUGHT]
Step 1 — [First analysis step]
Step 2 — [Second analysis step]
Step 3 — [Building to the output]

[TECHNIQUE 5: SELF-EVALUATION]
Step N — QUALITY CHECK: Review your output against [criteria].
Fix anything that [specific quality bar].

###OUTPUT FORMAT###
[TECHNIQUE 6: OUTPUT PRIMER]
[Exact structure of the expected response]

###CONSTRAINTS###
[Hard rules the model must follow]

###EXAMPLES### (optional)
[TECHNIQUE 3: FEW-SHOT]
Good: [example of what you want]
Bad: [example of what you don't want]
```

### Real Example: Using the Framework

Let's say you need to write a cold email to a potential client. Here's how a beginner, intermediate, and advanced prompt engineer would approach it:

**Beginner:**
```
Write a cold email to a potential client for my web design business.
```

**Intermediate (uses 2-3 techniques):**
```
You are a B2B sales copywriter. Write a cold email for my web
design business targeting e-commerce brands doing $1-5M/year.

Keep it under 100 words. No "I hope this finds you well." Lead
with their problem, not my services. End with a specific CTA.
```

**Advanced (uses 5+ techniques):**
```
<system>
You are a cold email specialist who has written outreach sequences
with 15%+ reply rates. You know that cold emails fail when they
talk about the sender. The first line must be about the RECIPIENT's
world — something specific enough that they know this isn't a mass
email. You follow the PAS framework: Problem, Agitate, Solve.
</system>

###TASK###
Write a cold email for my web design business.

###CONTEXT###
My business: Custom web design for e-commerce brands
Target: E-commerce brand owners doing $1-5M/year revenue
Common problem: Their site looks outdated, loads slowly, and
converts at 1-2% when the industry average is 3-4%
My differentiator: I redesign sites in 2 weeks (competitors take 2-3 months)

###PROCESS###
Step 1 — Identify the specific pain point that would make this
prospect stop scrolling in their inbox. What's costing them money
RIGHT NOW?

Step 2 — Write 3 versions:
a) Lead with a specific observation about their current site
b) Lead with an industry stat that creates urgency
c) Lead with a peer comparison ("Your competitor [X] just did this...")

Step 3 — For each version, check:
- Is the first sentence about THEM, not me? (If not, rewrite)
- Is there a specific number or detail? (If not, add one)
- Would I reply to this if I received it? (If not, sharpen it)
- Is it under 80 words? (If not, cut)

###CONSTRAINTS###
- Under 80 words (short emails get more replies)
- No "I hope this finds you well" or "I came across your website"
- CTA must be a specific question, not "let me know if interested"
- Subject line under 40 characters

###EXAMPLE###
Good subject line: "your checkout page"
Good first line: "I bought a [product] from your site last week.
The product was great — but the checkout took 4 clicks too many."

Bad subject line: "Partnership opportunity"
Bad first line: "Hi, my name is [X] and I run a web design agency..."
```

The advanced version will produce dramatically better output — not because it's longer, but because every element gives the model specific, useful guidance.

## Common Mistakes (And How to Fix Them)

### Mistake 1: Being Vague
**Bad:** "Write something about AI for my audience"
**Good:** "Write a 200-word LinkedIn post about how AI saves freelancers 5+ hours/week, targeting solo consultants making $5-15K/month, in a direct and practical tone"

The fix: answer WHO, WHAT, WHY, HOW LONG, and WHAT TONE before you hit enter.

### Mistake 2: Not Including Context
**Bad:** "Improve this email" + [email text]
**Good:** "Improve this email. Context: this is a follow-up to a client who hasn't responded in 2 weeks. They're a long-term client. I want to be warm but direct. The goal is to get a call scheduled by Friday."

The fix: always include the situation, relationship, and desired outcome.

### Mistake 3: Accepting the First Output
The first response is a first draft. Always iterate:
- "Make the opening more specific"
- "The tone is too formal, make it sound more like a text to a friend"
- "Cut this in half — keep only the most essential points"
- "Now critique this from the perspective of a skeptical reader"

The fix: treat AI like a collaborative partner, not a vending machine.

### Mistake 4: Using the Same Prompt for Everything
ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini have different strengths:
- **Claude** — best for long-form writing, analysis, and nuanced reasoning
- **ChatGPT** — best for creative brainstorming, code generation, and general tasks
- **Gemini** — best for tasks involving real-time data, search, and Google ecosystem integration

The fix: match the model to the task, and adjust your prompting style accordingly.

### Mistake 5: Writing Prompts That Are Too Long
More words ≠ better prompts. The goal is PRECISION, not length. A focused 50-word prompt often beats a rambling 500-word one.

The fix: every sentence in your prompt should serve a purpose. If you can remove it without changing the output, remove it.

## The 10 Rules of Prompt Engineering

These are the principles that guide everything above. Memorize these and you'll naturally write better prompts:

1. **Specificity beats length.** A precise 3-sentence prompt beats a vague 3-paragraph prompt.

2. **Show, don't tell.** One good example communicates more than a paragraph of instructions.

3. **Think before you ask.** If you don't know what you want, the AI definitely doesn't.

4. **The model mirrors your effort.** Lazy input produces lazy output. Thoughtful input produces thoughtful output.

5. **Constraints improve creativity.** "Write a LinkedIn post" produces generic content. "Write a LinkedIn post under 150 words that opens with a specific number and ends with a question" produces something worth reading.

6. **Iterate, don't restart.** The second or third exchange is almost always better than rewriting the whole prompt.

7. **Context is king.** The more relevant context you provide, the less the model guesses. Every guess is a potential miss.

8. **Break complex tasks into steps.** Don't ask for a complete marketing strategy in one prompt. Ask for the audience analysis first, then the channel strategy, then the content plan.

9. **The persona is the lens.** Changing who the AI "is" changes everything about what it produces. Experiment with different expert roles.

10. **Save what works.** When a prompt produces great output, save it. Building a personal prompt library is the highest-leverage habit you can develop.

## Your Prompt Engineering Starter Kit

Here's how to start using these techniques today:

**Level 1 — Start here (add to every prompt):**
- Give a clear role: "You are a [specific expert]..."
- Be specific about what you want: audience, tone, length, format
- Include at least one constraint: "No jargon" or "Under 200 words"

**Level 2 — Add when the task is important:**
- Use structured sections (###INPUT###, ###OUTPUT###)
- Add chain-of-thought: "Before writing, think through..."
- Include a self-evaluation step: "Review your output and check..."

**Level 3 — Use for high-stakes tasks:**
- Full persona with philosophy and domain knowledge
- Multi-step reasoning process
- Few-shot examples of good AND bad output
- Self-critique with specific quality criteria
- Output format template

You don't jump to Level 3 for everything. Quick questions get Level 1. Client proposals get Level 3. Match the engineering effort to the importance of the task.
