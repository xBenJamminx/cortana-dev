# Brand Naming Reference

## Name Types

### Descriptive
Directly describes what the company does or offers.
- **Pros**: Immediately clear, easy to understand, good for SEO
- **Cons**: Hard to trademark, limits future pivots, often forgettable
- **Examples**: General Electric, Toys "R" Us, The Container Store, General Motors

### Suggestive
Evokes qualities or benefits without stating them directly.
- **Pros**: Memorable, ownable, creates intrigue, allows brand building
- **Cons**: Requires more marketing investment to establish meaning
- **Examples**: Pinterest (pin + interest), Netflix (internet + flicks), Instagram (instant + telegram), Slack

### Abstract/Invented
Completely made-up words with no inherent meaning.
- **Pros**: Highly distinctive, easy to trademark, blank canvas for meaning
- **Cons**: Requires significant investment to build associations, can feel cold
- **Examples**: Kodak, Xerox, Häagen-Dazs, Verizon, Accenture

### Acronym
Initials or abbreviated forms of longer names.
- **Pros**: Short, memorable once established
- **Cons**: Hard to establish initially, often lack personality, overused in B2B
- **Examples**: IBM, BMW, UPS, ASOS (As Seen On Screen)

### Founder/Place Names
Named after founders, locations, or other proper nouns.
- **Pros**: Personal connection, heritage, authenticity
- **Cons**: May limit growth, succession issues, can feel dated
- **Examples**: Ford, Disney, Amazon (river), Patagonia (region)

### Metaphorical
Borrowed from an unrelated domain to suggest qualities.
- **Pros**: Evocative, memorable, rich with associations
- **Cons**: Meaning may not be immediately clear, requires brand building
- **Examples**: Apple, Jaguar, Puma, Amazon, Caterpillar

## The SMILE Criteria

Evaluate name candidates against these five factors:

### Suggestive
Does the name evoke something relevant about the brand, product, or experience?
- Strong: Slack (suggests ease, casual), Sprint (suggests speed)
- Weak: Generic terms that could apply to anything

### Meaningful
Does it resonate with the target audience's values, needs, or aspirations?
- Consider cultural context and audience demographics
- Test for positive associations within the target market

### Imagery
Does it create a mental picture or evoke sensory experience?
- Names that paint pictures are more memorable
- Abstract names can still evoke imagery through sound symbolism

### Legs
Can it extend as the brand grows into new products, markets, or categories?
- Avoid names too specific to current offering
- Consider: What if this brand expands internationally? Into adjacent categories?

### Emotional
Does it trigger an emotional response? Does it feel like something?
- The best names make people feel something immediately
- Test gut reactions—first impressions matter

## Sound Symbolism

The sounds in a name affect perception independent of meaning.

### Consonant Effects
| Sound | Associated Qualities |
|-------|---------------------|
| K, T, P (plosives) | Sharp, powerful, quick, modern |
| S, F, V (fricatives) | Smooth, flowing, sophisticated |
| M, N (nasals) | Soft, warm, nurturing |
| B, D, G (voiced stops) | Solid, grounded, substantial |
| L, R (liquids) | Flowing, elegant, premium |

### Vowel Effects
| Sound | Associated Qualities |
|-------|---------------------|
| A (as in "cat") | Open, accessible, friendly |
| E (as in "see") | Sharp, precise, technical |
| I (as in "high") | Light, bright, innovative |
| O (as in "go") | Round, full, substantial |
| U (as in "blue") | Deep, serious, premium |

### Structural Considerations
- **Short names** (1-2 syllables): More memorable, feel modern and tech-forward
- **Longer names** (3+ syllables): Can feel more established, premium, or traditional
- **Rhythm**: Names with stressed/unstressed patterns are easier to say and remember

## Evaluation Process

### Round 1: Generate Broadly
- Aim for 50-100 candidates across multiple name types
- Don't self-edit too early—include wild ideas
- Use word association, thesaurus exploration, foreign language inspiration
- Consider: metaphors, compound words, word blends, invented words

### Round 2: Initial Screen
Filter out names that:
- Are too generic or descriptive to trademark
- Have obvious negative associations
- Are too difficult to pronounce or spell
- Are too similar to major competitors
- Don't pass a quick Google search (obvious conflicts)

### Round 3: SMILE Evaluation
Score remaining candidates (1-5) on each SMILE criterion. Calculate weighted totals based on brand priorities.

### Round 4: Practical Viability
For top candidates, check:
- **Domain availability**: Exact match .com, or viable alternatives
- **Social handle availability**: Key platforms
- **Trademark preliminary search**: USPTO, international databases
- **Linguistic screening**: Meaning in major world languages, especially target markets
- **Competitive conflicts**: Similar names in adjacent industries

### Round 5: Contextual Testing
Place top 3-5 names in context:
- How does it look in a logo lockup?
- How does it sound in a sentence? ("I just ordered from X")
- How does it sound when answered by phone? ("Thank you for calling X")
- Does it work as a verb or noun? ("Just X it")

## Common Naming Pitfalls

**Avoid these traps:**

1. **Design-by-committee consensus**: The most popular name is often the safest (blandest). Great names are polarizing.

2. **Over-describing**: Names that explain everything leave no room for brand building.

3. **Trend-chasing**: Names that sound "of the moment" age quickly (e.g., adding "-ly" or "-ify").

4. **Ignoring sound**: A name might look good on paper but be awkward to say.

5. **Premature attachment**: Don't fall in love with a name before checking availability.

6. **Linguistic landmines**: Always check meanings in other languages. (Classic example: Chevy Nova in Spanish markets, where "no va" = "doesn't go")

7. **Overthinking**: At some point, the name is just a vessel. Nike, Google, and Amazon were meaningless until the brands gave them meaning.

---

## Presentation Tips

When presenting name options:

- **Show names in consistent, neutral typography** to focus evaluation on the name itself, not design treatment
- **Provide context visuals** (mood imagery related to the brand) rather than logo concepts
- **Lead with your recommendation** in co-pilot mode
- **Present in small batches** (3-5 at a time) in guided mode to avoid overwhelm
- **Never present a name you wouldn't be proud of**—the client will always pick that one
